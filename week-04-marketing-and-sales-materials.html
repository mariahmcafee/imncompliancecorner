Marketing and Sales Materials

What triggers the CMS marketing classification, how the carrier and CMS approval process works, and the disclaimers and prohibitions that apply to every piece you produce.

What Triggers the Marketing Classification

CMS classifies a material as marketing when it meets either of two criteria:

  1. 1
    ContentThe material mentions any plan benefit, including dental, vision, hearing, transportation, meal benefits, OTC allowances, premium reductions, cost savings, or star ratings.
  2. 2
    IntentThe material is designed to draw a beneficiary toward enrollment in a specific plan or to influence their coverage decision.
Important: A material does not need to name a specific plan or carrier to be classified as marketing. Generic benefit mentions trigger the requirement. The format does not matter — a social media post, a mailer, a flyer, a website page, and a text message are all subject to the same rules.

The CMS Approval Process

All marketing materials must be approved before use. The approval process:

  1. 1
    Create the materialConduct an initial self-review against CMS requirements.
  2. 2
    Submit for reviewSubmit to your FMO or carrier compliance team for review.
  3. 3
    Carrier reviewThe carrier reviews the material and either approves it, requests changes, or rejects it.
  4. 4
    FMO submission to CMSOnce changes are made, your FMO submits the material to CMS via the Health Plan Management System (HPMS) for the carrier to "opt-in."
  5. 5
    Approval confirmationYou receive approval confirmation and may begin using the material.
Timeline: The full process typically takes 6 to 8 weeks. Start the approval process early for AEP materials — no later than June 30th — to ensure approval in time for AEP usage.

Required Elements of Marketing Materials

Materials must include the following where applicable:

  • TPMO disclaimer
  • Phone number disclaimer: any material listing a phone number connecting to a licensed agent must include "Calling this number will connect you to a licensed insurance agent"
  • Compliant agent title from the CMS-approved list
  • Accurate plan year and coverage area information
  • No language implying government endorsement or Medicare affiliation

Materials must not include:

  • The Medicare or CMS name, logo, or imagery in a way implying government endorsement
  • Savings claims based on individual circumstances rather than plan-level data
  • Plan benefit information for areas where those benefits are not actually available

Comparing Plans and Carriers

Comparing one carrier's plan to another carrier's plan by name in marketing materials requires written consent from all carriers involved in the comparison. Without that consent, named carrier comparisons are not permitted.

General comparisons of plan types (HMO vs. PPO) that do not name specific carriers are permitted without carrier consent. Star ratings comparisons must use current CMS-published ratings and cannot be presented in a misleading context.

Government Endorsement Prohibition

Section 1140 of the Social Security Act prohibits using the words Medicare, CMS, Department of Health and Human Services, or related terms in a way that implies government endorsement of your products or services.

  • Do not describe yourself as working for Medicare
  • Do not use the CMS logo, or an image of a Medicare ID card, on any materials
  • Cannot use titles such as Medicare Expert or Medicare Specialist
  • Do not design materials that look like official government communications
Action item: Audit your website, social profiles, business cards, and ad creative for any language or imagery that could create this impression.

CY2027 Update — Effective October 1, 2026

Starting October 1, 2026, agents may use superlative language such as best, most, and top-rated in marketing materials without needing supporting documentation on file. The underlying prohibition on misleading statements still applies — this change covers documentation requirements, not accuracy standards.

CMS is also increasing oversight of how supplemental benefits such as flex card and debit card-style benefits are described in marketing materials. Before marketing any supplemental benefit, verify the current benefit details directly from carrier materials and avoid generalizations about availability or dollar amounts.
